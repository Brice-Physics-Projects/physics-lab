# Physics Self-Study Roadmap

> A structured, long-term journey through physics designed for engineers, software developers, and lifelong learners who want to understand **how the universe works** from first principles.

---

## 🎯 Purpose

This repository is my personal roadmap for studying physics.

Rather than pursuing another degree, my goal is to build a solid conceptual and mathematical understanding of the major areas of physics through self-study. The emphasis is on understanding the **why** behind the equations, developing intuition, and exploring the ideas that have shaped our understanding of reality.

As a civil engineer with an interest in applied mathematics, machine learning, and infrastructure analytics, I've found myself increasingly drawn toward the deeper questions physics attempts to answer:

- Why is the speed of light constant?
- What is spacetime?
- Why does entropy always increase?
- How does quantum mechanics describe reality?
- What happens inside a black hole?
- Is information fundamental to the universe?

This repository serves as both a study guide and a record of that journey.

---

## 📚 Goals

- Develop a strong foundation in undergraduate-level physics.
- Strengthen the mathematical tools used throughout physics.
- Build intuition before focusing on advanced derivations.
- Connect concepts across physics, engineering, mathematics, and computer science.
- Explore modern topics such as quantum information, cosmology, and black holes.

# 🗺️ Learning Roadmap

## Phase 1 — Mathematical Methods

Build the mathematical language used throughout physics.

- Algebra
- Trigonometry
- Calculus I
- Calculus II
- Calculus III
- Differential Equations
- Linear Algebra
- Vector Calculus
- Complex Numbers
- Probability & Statistics
- Introduction to Tensors

---

## Phase 2 — Classical Mechanics

Understand motion and the laws governing the physical world.

Topics include:

- Motion
- Newton's Laws
- Forces
- Work & Energy
- Conservation of Energy
- Momentum
- Rotational Motion
- Oscillations
- Gravitation

---

## Phase 3 — Electricity & Magnetism

Study electric and magnetic fields and their unification.

Topics include:

- Electric Charge
- Electric Fields
- Electric Potential
- Capacitance
- Current & Resistance
- Magnetic Fields
- Electromagnetic Induction
- Maxwell's Equations (Conceptual)
- Electromagnetic Waves

---

## Phase 4 — Special Relativity

Learn how space and time change at high velocities.

Topics include:

- Why the Speed of Light is Constant
- Spacetime
- Lorentz Transformations
- Time Dilation
- Length Contraction
- Relativistic Momentum
- Mass-Energy Equivalence

---

## Phase 5 — Quantum Mechanics

Explore the probabilistic nature of reality.

Topics include:

- Wave-Particle Duality
- Wavefunctions
- Probability Amplitudes
- Schrödinger Equation (Conceptual)
- Quantum States
- Measurement
- Spin
- Quantum Tunneling
- Entanglement

---

## Phase 6 — Statistical Mechanics

Study how microscopic behavior gives rise to macroscopic laws.

Topics include:

- Microstates & Macrostates
- Entropy
- Temperature
- Probability Distributions
- Boltzmann Distribution
- Information Theory
- Arrow of Time

---

## Phase 7 — General Relativity

Study gravity as the geometry of spacetime.

Topics include:

- Equivalence Principle
- Curved Spacetime
- Geodesics
- Einstein's Field Equations (Conceptual)
- Gravitational Lensing
- Black Holes
- Gravitational Waves
- Cosmology
- Expansion of the Universe
- Big Bang

---

## Phase 8 — Modern Physics & Open Questions

Explore some of today's biggest unanswered questions.

Topics include:

- Black Hole Information Paradox
- Holographic Principle
- Quantum Field Theory (Conceptual)
- Dark Matter
- Dark Energy
- Cosmic Inflation
- String Theory (Overview)
- Loop Quantum Gravity (Overview)
- Quantum Computing
- Emergent Spacetime
- Simulation Hypothesis (Physics vs. Philosophy)

# 💭 Philosophy

The objective isn't to become a professional physicist.

The objective is to better understand the physical laws that govern our universe and to become a more thoughtful engineer, programmer, and lifelong learner.

As Richard Feynman famously said:

> "I think I can safely say that nobody understands quantum mechanics."

That probably won't stop me from trying.
